01

Introduction to Semiconductors

A semiconductor is a material whose electrical conductivity lies between that of a conductor and an insulator. Unlike metals, the resistivity of a semiconductor decreases with increasing temperature. Semiconductors are the backbone of modern electronics — transistors, diodes, solar cells, and LEDs all rely on them.

1.1 Energy Band Theory

When ~10²³ atoms form a crystal, discrete atomic energy levels broaden into continuous energy bands. The gap between bands determines whether a material conducts or insulates.

Key Energy Bands
  • Valence Band (VB): Highest filled band at 0 K — electrons here are bound to atoms
  • Conduction Band (CB): Next higher band — electrons here move freely and conduct
  • Band Gap (Eᵍ): Forbidden energy region between VB and CB — its width classifies the material
  • Fermi Level (EF): Highest energy level an electron can occupy at absolute zero (0 K)
MaterialBand StructureBand GapResistivity (Ω·cm)Example
ConductorVB and CB overlap~0 eV10⁻⁶ – 10⁻³Cu, Al, Ag
SemiconductorSmall gap between VB & CB0.1 – 3 eV10⁻³ – 10⁸Si, Ge, GaAs
InsulatorLarge gap between VB & CB> 3 eV10⁸ – 10¹⁶Diamond, SiO₂

1.2 Fermi Level Position

Intrinsic SC

  • Fermi level at midgap
  • Exactly between VB & CB
  • n = p = nᵢ

N-Type SC

  • Fermi level near CB
  • Closer to conduction band
  • n ≫ p

P-Type SC

  • Fermi level near VB
  • Closer to valence band
  • p ≫ n
02

Types of Semiconductors

2.1 Intrinsic (Pure) Semiconductors

An intrinsic semiconductor is a chemically pure semiconductor with no intentional impurities. Its electrical properties are governed entirely by the material itself.

Key Properties
  • Examples: Pure Silicon (Si), Pure Germanium (Ge)
  • At room temperature: number of electrons = number of holes → n = p = nᵢ
  • Carrier concentration increases exponentially with temperature
  • Very low conductivity at room temperature
  • Fermi level lies exactly in the middle of the band gap
  • Both electrons (CB) and holes (VB) act as charge carriers
  • Electrical neutrality maintained at all times

2.2 Extrinsic (Doped) Semiconductors

An extrinsic semiconductor is obtained by adding controlled amounts of impurity atoms (dopants) into an intrinsic semiconductor — a process called doping. Doping drastically increases conductivity without breaking covalent bonds.

N-Type Semiconductor
  • Dopant: Pentavalent (5 valence e⁻) — P, As, Sb
  • Majority carriers: Electrons (e⁻)
  • Minority carriers: Holes (h⁺)
  • Fermi level: Near conduction band
  • Donor atom donates 1 free electron
  • 5th electron is superfluous → enters CB
  • Hall coefficient: RH < 0 (negative)
  • Net charge: Electrically neutral overall
P-Type Semiconductor
  • Dopant: Trivalent (3 valence e⁻) — B, Ga, In
  • Majority carriers: Holes (h⁺)
  • Minority carriers: Electrons (e⁻)
  • Fermi level: Near valence band
  • Acceptor atom accepts 1 electron → creates hole
  • Deficiency of one covalent bond = hole
  • Hall coefficient: RH > 0 (positive)
  • Net charge: Electrically neutral overall
Important: Doped semiconductors remain electrically neutral overall. "N-type" and "P-type" refer to the dominant mobile charge carriers, NOT the net charge of the material.

Direct & Indirect Band Gap Semiconductors

In a crystal, the energy-momentum (E-K) relationship depends on crystal direction. This leads to two types of band gaps based on the alignment of conduction band minimum and valence band maximum in momentum (k-vector) space.

FeatureDirect Band GapIndirect Band Gap
CB min & VB maxSame k-value (same momentum)Different k-values (different momentum)
Electron transitionDirectly emits a photonRequires phonon (lattice vibration) assistance
Light emissionEfficient — emits photons readilyInefficient — energy released as heat (phonons)
Suitable forLEDs, Laser diodes, PhotodetectorsTransistors, Si-based electronics
ExamplesGaAs, InAs, GaN, InGaN, Amorphous SiCrystalline Si, Ge, AlSb
Band gap at 300KGaAs = 1.42 eV, GaN = 3.4 eVSi = 1.12 eV, Ge = 0.66 eV
Photon emissionDirect — high quantum efficiencyVia phonon — low quantum efficiency
Why Si and Ge cannot be used for LEDs: Both have indirect band gaps. Electron transitions require phonon assistance and energy is lost as heat rather than emitted as photons. Direct band gap materials (GaAs, GaN, InGaN) are used for LEDs and lasers.
Photon Emission Condition
hν ≥ Eᵍ (photon energy must exceed band gap)
In indirect gap: additional phonon with momentum ħq is needed → transition probability much lower

Carrier Concentration & Conductivity

4.1 Carrier Concentration in Intrinsic Semiconductors

In an intrinsic semiconductor, thermally generated electron-hole pairs maintain charge neutrality: n = p = nᵢ, where nᵢ is the intrinsic carrier concentration.

Electron Concentration
n = 2(2πm*ₑkT/h²)^(3/2) · exp((E_F − E_C)/kT)
m*ₑ = effective mass of electron, k = Boltzmann constant, T = temperature (K)
Hole Concentration
p = 2(2πm*ₕkT/h²)^(3/2) · exp((E_V − E_F)/kT)
m*ₕ = effective mass of hole, E_V = valence band energy
Intrinsic Carrier Concentration (nᵢ)
nᵢ = 2(2πkT/h²)^(3/2) · (m*ₑ m*ₕ)^(3/4) · exp(−Eᵍ/2kT)
Mass-Action Law: n · p = nᵢ² (valid for intrinsic and extrinsic)
Key Observations
  • nᵢ increases exponentially with temperature (SC becomes more conductive when heated)
  • At room temperature: nᵢ(Si) ≈ 1.5×10¹⁰ cm⁻³, nᵢ(Ge) ≈ 2.4×10¹³ cm⁻³
  • Mass-Action Law: n·p = nᵢ² — holds for both intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors
  • Larger band gap → smaller nᵢ → less intrinsic conductivity at room temperature

4.2 Electrical Conductivity

Electrical conductivity (σ) quantifies how well a material conducts electricity. For a semiconductor, both electrons and holes contribute:

General Conductivity
σ = n·e·μₑ + p·e·μₕ
μₑ, μₕ = electron and hole mobility (cm²/V·s); e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C
For Intrinsic Semiconductor (n = p = nᵢ)
σ = nᵢ · e · (μₑ + μₕ) = A · exp(−Eᵍ/2kT)
A = pre-exponential constant
Logarithmic Form (for Eᵍ Determination)
ln σ = ln A − Eᵍ/(2kT)
Plot ln σ vs 1/T → straight line with slope = −Eᵍ/2k → find band gap experimentally

Mobility (μ) is the measure of how fast a charge carrier moves through a semiconductor under unit electric field. It is affected by temperature and doping concentration. μₑ > μₕ for most semiconductors (electrons are lighter).

05

Hall Effect & Applications

Definition — Hall Effect
  • When a current-carrying conductor (or semiconductor) is placed in a transverse magnetic field,
  • An electric field is produced perpendicular to BOTH the current and the magnetic field.
  • The generated transverse voltage is called the Hall Voltage (VH).
  • The generated transverse electric field is called the Hall Electric Field (EH).

5.1 Theory & Derivation

With current in x-direction and magnetic field B in z-direction, carriers experience a Lorentz force that deflects them. Charge accumulates until equilibrium is reached:

Equilibrium Condition
eE_H = Bev → E_H = Bv = BJ/(ne) = BJR_H
J = current density, n = carrier density, v = carrier velocity
Hall Coefficient
R_H = E_H / (B·J) = 1/(pe) for p-type | R_H = −1/(ne) for n-type
Sign of R_H directly identifies semiconductor type
Measured Hall Voltage & Hall Coefficient
V_H = R_H · B · I / t → R_H = V_H · t / (B · I)
t = sample thickness, I = current, B = magnetic flux density

5.2 Applications of Hall Effect

ApplicationMethodFormula
i) Type of SemiconductorN-type: R_H negative; P-type: R_H positive. Direction of Hall voltage reveals type.Sign of R_H
ii) Carrier ConcentrationMeasure R_H experimentally, then calculate n or p directlyn = 1/(e·|R_H|)
iii) Carrier MobilityCombine measured conductivity (σ) with Hall coefficientμ = |R_H| · σ = σ/(ne)
iv) Magnetic Flux DensityUse a semiconductor sample of known R_H to measure unknown BB = V_H·t / (I·R_H)

P-N Junction Diode

6.1 Formation

A P-N junction forms when p-type and n-type regions of the same semiconductor material are brought into metallurgical contact (homojunction).

Formation Process
  • Holes diffuse p→n; electrons diffuse n→p (concentration gradient)
  • Near junction: carriers recombine, leaving ionized impurity atoms (space charge)
  • This creates the Depletion Region / Space Charge Region (SCR)
  • Built-in electric field points from N to P across the depletion region
  • Equilibrium: diffusion current = drift current → zero net current
  • Built-in potential Vbi opposes further diffusion
Built-in Potential
V_bi = (kT/q) · ln(N_A · N_D / nᵢ²)
Si ~0.6 V, Ge <0.66 V, GaAs ~1.2 V at 300K with N_A = N_D = 10¹⁵/cm³

6.2 Biasing Conditions

ConditionApplied VoltageBarrier HeightDepletion WidthCurrent
Zero BiasV_A = 0V_biW₀ (natural width)Zero net current
Forward BiasV_A > 0 (+to P)V_bi − V_A (reduces)DecreasesExponentially increases
Reverse BiasV_A < 0 (+to N)V_bi + |V_A| (increases)IncreasesVery small I₀ (saturation)

6.3 V-I Characteristics — Shockley Equation

Ideal Diode Equation (Shockley)
I = I₀ (e^(qV/kT) − 1) = I₀ (e^(V/V_T) − 1)
I₀ = reverse saturation current; V_T = kT/q = 0.026 V at 300K (thermal voltage)
V-I Characteristics Summary
  • Forward Bias: Current rises exponentially after cut-in voltage (~0.3V Ge, ~0.7V Si, ~1.2V GaAs)
  • Reverse Bias: Small constant reverse saturation current I₀ flows (minority carrier drift)
  • Breakdown Region: At very high reverse voltage, current suddenly increases sharply
  • Temperature: Forward voltage drops ~2 mV/°C; I₀ roughly doubles every ~10°C rise
  • The "knee" of the I-V curve marks onset of significant forward conduction

6.4 Breakdown Mechanisms

Zener Breakdown
  • Quantum tunneling of electrons across thin depletion region
  • Occurs in heavily doped junctions (thin depletion layer)
  • At lower reverse voltages (<5 V)
  • E = V/d is large even at low V (small d)
  • Field ionizes covalent bonds directly
Avalanche Breakdown
  • Carrier multiplication by impact ionization
  • Occurs in moderately doped junctions (wide depletion layer)
  • At higher reverse voltages (>5–6 V)
  • High-energy carriers collide with atoms → new e-h pairs → cascade
  • More voltage needed to build same field (large d)

Carrier Action in Semiconductors

TypeDefinitionDriving ForceCurrent Formula
1. Drift Motion of carriers in response to an applied electric field. Electrons move opposite to field; holes move along field direction. Electric field E J_drift = σE = (nμₑ + pμₕ)eE
2. Diffusion Carriers spread from regions of HIGH concentration to LOW concentration due to random thermal motion. Like perfume spreading in a room. Concentration gradient dn/dx J_diff = eDₑ(dn/dx) + eDₕ(dp/dx)
3. Recombination–Generation Recombination: e⁻ fills a hole, releasing energy. Generation: e-h pair created by absorbing thermal or optical energy. Thermal / optical energy Net rate R = (np − nᵢ²)/τ
Einstein's Relation (connects Diffusion & Mobility)
D/μ = kT/q = V_T ≈ 0.026 V at 300 K
D = diffusion coefficient (cm²/s), μ = mobility (cm²/V·s), V_T = thermal voltage

Optoelectronic Devices

Optoelectronic devices exploit the interaction between light (photons) and electric current in semiconductors. Three functional categories:

Photodetector

  • Light → Electric signal
  • Purpose: detect/measure light
  • Example: Photodiode

Solar Cell

  • Light → Electric power
  • Purpose: generate electricity
  • Example: PV cell

LED / Laser

  • Electricity → Light
  • Purpose: emit photons
  • Example: LED, Laser diode

8.1 Light Emitting Diode (LED)

An LED is a p-n junction diode that emits light when forward biased. Electrons from the n-side recombine with holes on the p-side in the depletion/active region, releasing energy as photons (electroluminescence).

Photon Energy & Wavelength
E = hν = hc/λ = Eᵍ → λ = hc/Eᵍ
h = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s, c = 3×10⁸ m/s; wavelength determines the colour of emitted light
MaterialColourWavelengthBand Gap (eV)
GaN (Gallium Nitride)Blue / UV~460 nm3.4
InGaN (Indium Gallium Nitride)Blue / Green / Violet450–530 nm2.3–3.4
AlGaInPRed / Orange / Yellow590–700 nm1.8–2.1
GaAsP (Gallium Arsenide Phosphide)Red~650 nm1.9
ZnSe (Zinc Selenide)Blue / Green~480 nm2.6
SiC (Silicon Carbide)Blue / UV~420 nm3.0
Advantages
  • Small size and lightweight
  • Long operational life
  • High switching speed (<1 ns)
  • Available in multiple spectral colours
  • High quantum efficiency
  • Low temperature dependence
  • Light output proportional to current (linear)
Disadvantages
  • Output power affected by temperature changes
  • Requires higher power than normal p-n diode
  • Low luminous efficiency
  • Overcurrent can permanently damage LED
  • Requires direct band gap material (no Si/Ge)
Why Si/Ge can't make LEDs: Indirect band gap → phonon-assisted transitions → energy lost as heat, not light. Direct band gap materials (GaAs, GaN) are required for efficient photon emission.

8.2 Photodiode

A photodiode is a p-n junction diode that converts incident light into electrical current. It operates under reverse bias.

Working Principle
  • Condition: photon energy hν > Eᵍ → excites electron from VB to CB, creating e-h pair
  • Required wavelength: λ < hc/Eᵍ = λ_cutoff
  • Built-in E-field at depletion region separates the generated e-h pair
  • Electrons drift to n-side; holes drift to p-side → photocurrent flows
  • Dark current (no light): very small reverse saturation current I₀
  • Illuminated current: I_photo ∝ incident light intensity
Cutoff Wavelength
λ_c = hc / Eᵍ
Photons with λ < λ_c are absorbed; photons with λ > λ_c pass through unabsorbed

8.3 PIN Photodiode

A PIN diode has an intrinsic (lightly doped) layer sandwiched between heavily doped p⁺ and n⁺ regions. This greatly improves performance.

FeaturePN PhotodiodePIN Photodiode
Intrinsic layerAbsentPresent (thick, lightly doped)
Depletion regionNarrowMuch wider (spans i-layer)
Response timeSlowerFaster
Junction capacitanceHigherLower
Reverse voltageHigher neededOnly ~5V needed
Spectral responseLimitedExtended (UV to IR)
Photon penetration: Short λ (UV) absorbed near surface; long λ (IR) penetrates deep. PIN structure with thick i-layer accommodates both efficiently.

8.4 Solar Cell (Photovoltaic Cell)

A solar cell converts sunlight into electrical energy using the photovoltaic effect. It is essentially a p-n junction optimized for light absorption and carrier collection.

Working Principle
  • Step 1: Photon absorption — hν > Eᵍ creates electron-hole pair
  • Step 2: Separation — built-in field sweeps electrons to n-side, holes to p-side
  • Step 3: Collection — carriers flow through external circuit (conventional current p→n)
  • Step 4: Power delivery — electrical power delivered to load
Solar Cell Current Equation
I = I_sc − I₀(e^(qV/kT) − 1)
I_sc = short-circuit current (∝ light intensity); I₀ = dark saturation current
Fill Factor
FF = P_max / (I_sc × V_oc) = (I_mp × V_mp) / (I_sc × V_oc)
I_mp, V_mp = current & voltage at max power point; Typical FF = 0.8–0.9
Solar Cell Efficiency
η = P_max / P_in = FF × I_sc × V_oc / P_in
P_in = incident solar radiation power (W/m²); Standard: 1000 W/m²
ParameterDefinitionCondition
Short-circuit current (I_sc)Maximum current from cellOutput voltage = 0
Open-circuit voltage (V_oc)Maximum voltage from cellOutput current = 0
Maximum power (P_max)Maximum power deliverableAt optimum load
Fill Factor (FF)"Squareness" of I-V curve0.8–0.9 typical

8.5 Semiconductor Laser Diode

A semiconductor laser emits coherent, monochromatic, collimated light through stimulated emission. Requires forward bias above a threshold current.

Working Principle
  • Requires heavily doped (degenerate) p and n regions
  • Heavy forward bias creates population inversion in active region
  • A photon triggers recombination → produces an identical photon (coherent — same phase, frequency, direction)
  • Cleaved crystal facets (~30% reflectivity) form a Fabry-Perot optical cavity
  • Photons bounce back and forth, amplifying → laser action above threshold
  • Below threshold: spontaneous emission (like LED); above threshold: stimulated emission (laser)

Solved Numerical Problems

Problem 1 — Hall Effect (P-type identification)
A semiconductor slab carries I = 20 mA, thickness t = 1 mm, Hall voltage V_H = 37 μV, magnetic flux density B = 0.5 Wb/m². Find Hall coefficient and type.
Given: V_H = 37×10⁻⁶ V, t = 1×10⁻³ m, I = 20×10⁻³ A, B = 0.5 T
R_H = V_H·t / (B·I) = (37×10⁻⁶ × 1×10⁻³) / (20×10⁻³ × 0.5)
R_H = 37×10⁻⁹ / 10×10⁻³ = 3.7×10⁻⁶ C⁻¹m³
R_H = +3.7×10⁻⁶ C⁻¹m³ → POSITIVE → P-type semiconductor
Problem 2 — Hall Effect (N-type identification)
t = 0.2 cm, B = 0.3 T, I = 3 A, V_H = −0.015 V. Find R_H and semiconductor type.
Given: t = 0.2 cm = 0.2×10⁻² m, B = 0.3 T, I = 3 A, V_H = −0.015 V
R_H = (−0.015 × 0.2×10⁻²) / (0.3 × 3) = −3×10⁻⁵ / 0.9 = −3.33×10⁻⁵ m³/C
= −0.0033 cm³/C
R_H = −0.0033 cm³/C → NEGATIVE → N-type semiconductor
Problem 3 — Hall Effect (P-type, t = 0.1 cm)
t = 0.1 cm, B = 0.5 T, I = 2 A, V_H = 0.01 V. Find R_H and type.
R_H = V_H·t / (B·I) = (0.01 × 0.1×10⁻²) / (0.5 × 2)
R_H = 10⁻⁵ / 1 = 0.001 cm³/C
R_H = +0.001 cm³/C → POSITIVE → P-type semiconductor
Problem 4 — LED: Photon Energy
Calculate the energy of a photon emitted by an LED with wavelength λ = 500 nm.
E = hc/λ = (6.626×10⁻³⁴ × 3×10⁸) / (500×10⁻⁹)
E = 19.878×10⁻²⁶ / 5×10⁻⁷ = 3.98×10⁻¹⁹ J
E ≈ 3.98×10⁻¹⁹ J ≈ 2.49 eV (visible green-blue light)
Problem 5 — LED: Forward Voltage Drop
An LED has forward current I_f = 20 mA and power dissipation P = 100 mW. Find forward voltage V_f.
V_f = P / I_f = 100×10⁻³ / 20×10⁻³
V_f = 5 V
Problem 6 — Semiconductor Laser: Wavelength
Calculate the wavelength of light emitted by a semiconductor laser with band gap Eᵍ = 1.2 eV.
λ = hc / Eᵍ = (6.626×10⁻³⁴ × 3×10⁸) / (1.2 × 1.6×10⁻¹⁹)
λ = 19.878×10⁻²⁶ / 1.92×10⁻¹⁹ = 1.035×10⁻⁶ m
λ ≈ 1.04 μm (near-infrared — optical fibre communications)
Problem 7 — Solar Cell: Fill Factor, Max Power, Area
V_oc = 0.5 V, I_sc = 0.8 A, FF = 0.85. (a) Find P_max. (b) If V_mp = 0.4 V, find I_mp. (c) η = 10%, beam = 800 W/m², diffuse = 5% of beam. Find minimum cell area.
(a) P_max = FF × I_sc × V_oc = 0.85 × 0.8 × 0.5 = 0.34 W
(b) I_mp = P_max / V_mp = 0.34 / 0.4 = 0.85 A
(c) I_total = I_beam + I_diffuse = 800 + 800×0.05 = 840 W/m²
P_max = I_total × A_cell × η → 0.34 = 840 × A × 0.1 → A = 0.34/84
A_cell ≈ 4.05×10⁻³ m² ≈ 40.5 cm²
